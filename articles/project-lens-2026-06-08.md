# The Difference Between a Witness and an Affidavit

Centuries before TLS, the legal world had to settle the same question that signed-artifact standards have been quietly reshuffling through 2026: when does a piece of paper get to speak for itself?

A witness has to be present. An affidavit, once notarised and sworn, can be read into a record decades after the witness has died. The document carries its own warrant. You don't need to phone the issuer to find out whether the words on the page are still good.

This isn't a metaphor. It's a category — and most of the internet was not built in this category.

## What J.L. Austin saw, and what the internet missed

In *How to Do Things with Words*, Austin drew a line between two kinds of utterance. **Constative** sentences describe a state of affairs; they're true or false. **Performative** sentences *do* something — they commit, name, bet, bequeath. "I now pronounce you" doesn't report a marriage; it constitutes one.

Bearer instruments are the legal echo of the performative move. John Law's Mississippi Company issued 200,000 bearer shares in August 1717 — the first large-scale documents where possession alone was title. No register, no ledger lookup, no live attestation from the issuer. The paper *was* the claim, and whoever held the paper held the claim.

Most JSON on the open internet is constative. A response body describes a result. You trust the bytes because you trust the URL — and TLS authenticates the *pipe*, not the artifact. Once the bytes are saved to disk, they're orphaned. They need a return trip to defend themselves.

## 2026 is quietly building the affidavit layer

Different communities have spent the last eighteen months rediscovering the bearer-instrument move in different vocabularies.

- **C2PA Content Credentials** crossed 6,000 members in January, and every image generated by Adobe Firefly, OpenAI's DALL·E, Sora, and Google Imagen now ships with embedded provenance by default. The EU AI Act's Article 50 obligations enter force in August 2026, mandating machine-readable AI-content labels. The standard was also ratified as ISO/IEC 22144.
- **W3C Verifiable Credentials 2.0** became a W3C Recommendation in May 2025. The defining feature isn't the proof format; it's that verification can happen offline, without contacting the issuer in real time. The credential carries its own cryptographic warrant.
- **SLSA + Sigstore** went from a multi-week build-pipeline project two years ago to GitHub's default behaviour for public repositories. Artifact attestations achieve SLSA v1.0 Build Level 2 out of the box; the in-toto envelope (ITE-6) is becoming the lingua franca for "this thing came from where it claims it came from."

Three communities, three vocabularies, one structural move: lift the warrant off the connection and put it on the artifact.

## A small instance on a watched repo

MiroShark merged PR #152 this morning, twenty-four minutes after opening it: `GET /api/simulation/<id>/signed-result.json`. The endpoint wraps the canonical `signal.json` payload — direction, confidence, risk tier, quality health — in an HMAC-SHA256 envelope so an integrator can store a finished simulation's outcome locally and prove, months later, that the stored bytes match what the platform actually emitted.

The PR is 215 lines of service code and 499 lines of tests across 25 cases, with zero new dependencies — the project's forty-second consecutive zero-dep shipment. The signature covers the canonicalised inner result block, computed once and only once the deterministic way:

```python
canonical = json.dumps(result, sort_keys=True, separators=(",", ":"), ensure_ascii=True).encode("utf-8")
signature = hmac.new(WEBHOOK_SECRET.encode(), canonical, hashlib.sha256).hexdigest()
```

That single line is the affidavit clause. `sort_keys`, the compact separator pair, and ASCII-escaped Unicode pin the byte sequence so any recipient can reproduce the input to the HMAC exactly. The envelope's outer `signed_at` is wall-clock and deliberately excluded from the signed material — two consecutive requests for the same finished sim emit the same signature but different timestamps. The signature is hash-pinned to the outcome, not to the moment the bytes were served.

The PR names two real consumers behind the decision. Capacitr runs an `/x402/run` settlement flow and stores the outcome of each run; a signature lets the settlement record be audited months later without re-fetching the sim. AntFleet's `miroshark-bench` leaderboard wants to prove integrity to downstream consumers without phoning home for each row. The bytes have to outlast the service.

## The honest seam

HMAC is symmetric. Only parties who hold the `WEBHOOK_SECRET` can verify. That's not the bearer-instrument ideal — where *anyone* can verify — but a smaller circle: shared-secret bearer truth. The PR ships this deliberately. The same secret already authenticates the project's outbound webhook delivery, so existing integrators verify with the same `hmac.compare_digest` primitive they're already running. Zero new config, zero new key-distribution problem.

The envelope's `schema_version: "1"` and `algorithm: "hmac-sha256"` fields are the seam where a future Ed25519 variant would extend the verifying circle to "anyone with the public key." That's the philosophical interesting point: even partial detachment is a category change. The bytes can now defend themselves against the part of the future that doesn't include the originating server — which is the future that arrives by default, because servers go dark, companies fold, logs rotate, models retire.

## What the bearer-instrument instinct is correcting

Most internet infrastructure was built assuming the server outlives the artifact. The opposite is increasingly true. The 2026 signed-artifact wave — C2PA on images, SLSA on builds, VC 2.0 on credentials, single HMAC envelopes on JSON results — is the industry admitting, in four different vocabularies, that the artifact has to outlast the service.

The instinct isn't new. The Mississippi Company shareholders in 1717 already knew that a piece of paper good only when its issuer is reachable is not really a piece of paper at all. What's new is that JSON is starting to learn the same trick.

A signed result is not a louder result. It's a different category of object. It stops needing to be a witness, and starts being an affidavit.
