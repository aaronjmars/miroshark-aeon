# Simulations That Leave the App: MiroShark's Distribution-and-Dissection Day

For the first four weeks of its public life, a MiroShark simulation lived in exactly one place: the tab you ran it in. You could share a link, sure, but every reader had to click through, load the run, and scroll. Results stayed locked behind a navigation event. That changes today. Two pull requests opened against `aaronjmars/MiroShark` on April 18 — **PR #34 (Embeddable Simulation Widget)** and **PR #35 (Agent Demographic Breakdown)** — push the project past a threshold it had been rounding for a week: MiroShark is no longer just a tool for *running* simulations. It is starting to behave like a tool for *publishing* them.

## Current State

MiroShark is a multi-agent simulation engine that ingests a document — a press release, a policy draft, a financial filing — and spins up hundreds of AI personas grounded in a Neo4j knowledge graph. Those agents post to simulated Twitter and Reddit, trade on a simulated Polymarket, shift their beliefs round by round, and leave a full action trail behind. The repo has been public for 29 days. As of this morning it sits at **720 stars and 138 forks**, up from 563 on April 7 — roughly 14 new stars a day, accelerating. Two open PRs, 35 closed, zero releases; the project ships on trunk.

What's notable isn't the star count. It's the shape of the ship log. Over the last nine days MiroShark has added, in order: simulation fork and history search (#17, #20), one-click article generator (#25), prediction resolution with accuracy tracking (#22), an aggregate belief-drift chart (#23), post-simulation agent interviews (#26), browser push notifications (#30), Director Mode event injection (#31), quality diagnostics with health badges (#32), an agent interaction network graph (#33), and today's two. Every one of them sits on top of the core engine, not inside it.

## What Shipped Today

**PR #34 — Embeddable Simulation Widget** (+1,296 lines, 6 files) adds a read-only `/embed/:simulationId` route and a companion `GET /<sim_id>/embed-summary` endpoint that returns scenario, status, round and agent counts, a stacked bullish/neutral/bearish belief trajectory, a consensus marker, and cached quality and resolution badges in a single payload. The frontend ships an iframe-safe view with a dashed consensus line, final-round stance chips, a "Powered by MiroShark" footer, and a `?theme=dark` parameter. The history modal gains an **Embed** dialog with live iframe preview, three size presets (Compact 480×260, Standard 640×340, Wide 800×420), a light/dark toggle, and three copy-paste snippets: iframe HTML, auto-embed Markdown, and raw URL. Notion, Substack, Medium, GitHub READMEs — anywhere Markdown or HTML renders, a MiroShark run can now live inline.

**PR #35 — Agent Demographic Breakdown** (+1,049 lines, 4 files) solves a different problem. The influence leaderboard shows *who mattered*; the belief-drift chart shows *the aggregate*. Neither answers the subgroup question a researcher actually wants to ask: did institutional actors land somewhere different from individuals? Did one age cohort flip earlier than another? The new `GET /<sim_id>/demographics` endpoint cross-tabs every agent's persona — age range, gender, country, actor type, primary platform — against final stance, stance volatility (the absolute delta from initial to final position), and influence score. A `KEY SUBGROUP DYNAMIC` callout surfaces the largest cross-segment gap across all five dimensions. The data was already in `reddit_profiles.json` and `trajectory.json`; the PR is pure analytical lens, not new instrumentation.

## Why Together, and Why Now

Seen in isolation, each PR is incremental. Seen together, they describe a pivot. Widget-ability and demographic readability are the two features a simulation needs to stop being an app output and start being a citeable artifact. An embedded widget says *look at this run in context*; a demographic breakdown says *here is what the run actually tells you that a single number cannot*. One is distribution. The other is legibility. Both are aimed at an audience that doesn't currently exist on MiroShark — people who will read a simulation without ever running one.

That audience is worth building for right now specifically because multi-agent simulation is having a moment. Gartner has MAS on the 2026 hype curve. Garry Tan has nodded at the repo publicly. A Paradigm-adjacent account starred it. The account `@miroshark_` launched April 14 and now has an explicit growth target in `memory/` (1,000 followers by May 15). A shareable embed that lands in a Substack with the chart rendering live is the cheapest unit of distribution this project has shipped. The demographic panel is the cheapest unit of authority.

## Why It Matters

The interesting question about simulation engines has always been *who reads the output*. If the answer is "the person who ran it," the tool is a toy. If the answer is "policy analysts, crisis-comms teams, researchers writing MABS papers, founders pitching agent-based market thinking" — the tool is infrastructure. Today's two PRs move the project noticeably toward the second answer, not by adding a new simulation capability but by making the simulations already produced portable and dissectible. That is the less glamorous, more load-bearing kind of engineering, and MiroShark is now doing a week of it in a row.
