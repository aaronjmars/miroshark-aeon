# Week in Review: MiroShark Hardened Its Edges, Not Its Engine

*2026-06-15 — Weekly shipping update*

> Ten PRs merged this week and not one touched the simulation core — every change made a result easier to verify, consume, or contribute to from the outside.

## What Shipped

### Results you can prove without trusting the server
The week opened on the trust layer. `signed-result.json` (#152) wraps a simulation's signal payload — direction, confidence, risk tier, belief percentages, quality health — in an HMAC-SHA256-signed envelope, so an integrator can store a result locally and prove later, **offline**, that the bytes never changed. That's +1,051 lines spanning `backend/app/services/signed_result.py`, the simulation API, and the OpenAPI spec (7b79e7b). The honest caveat: the signature proves the payload is authentic, not that the simulation was correct — and it leans on a shared `WEBHOOK_SECRET`. It's tamper-evidence, not an oracle. Still, it's the difference between "trust our server" and "check it yourself."

Two days later `/api/activity.json` (#153) added a what-just-completed feed — the N most recently completed public sims, reverse-chronological, with `?limit=` (1–50, default 20), each entry carrying the small payload a polling consumer needs. That's the other half of consumability: discovery. At +1,615 lines it was the week's heaviest merge. Then `/api/surfaces.json` gained a `?type=<category>` filter (#157, 0a575db) so a consumer asks for one category — `?type=analytics` — instead of pulling the full catalog and filtering client-side. Three PRs, one direction: the output surface became machine-readable and checkable, not just viewable.

### The repo opened to outsiders
For the first time, a community PR landed. Daniel Andersen (#159) switched the frontend to same-origin API calls — path recognition now accepts `domain/api` instead of demanding a second hostname — and bumped Neo4j from 5.15 to 5.26, because the 5.15 build struggled with indexing. Not a drive-by typo fix; a real infra change from someone outside the core team, touching `docker-compose.yml`, `vite.config.js`, and the API client. That arrived alongside #162 (bda04b3), which turned `CONTRIBUTING.md` from a ~30-line test-only stub into a full guide: `npm run setup:all`, `.env` from the example, Neo4j via Docker Compose, and a walkthrough for adding an API endpoint. The repo stopped assuming you already knew how it worked — and the same day, someone who didn't, shipped.

### Read it in three languages
Localization filled out the week's reach. A full Japanese README (#156, 63cf725) joined the Simplified Chinese one (#155, +151 lines), and the language switcher now puts EN / 中文 / 日本語 one click apart from any of the three. The Chinese install guide (`docs/INSTALL.zh-CN.md`) rode in with #159. None of this is the product — but it decides who can find the product. With first JP coverage and a CN community already circling, three-language onboarding is the cheapest lever on the next hundred runs.

## Fixes & Polish
- **Round-2 code-quality sweep (#163)** — eight parallel audits across isolated worktrees over the ~56 commits since the round-1 cleanup; +1,368 / −317, each change verified behavior-preserving (1a35ba2).
- **Shared-helper dedup (#164)** — collapsed eight byte-identical ISO-8601 timestamp helpers into one `utc_iso8601()`, plus `avg_position` and public-base-url helpers across 19 files, and fixed a transcript boolean bug in passing (20aa674).
- **Green `main` (#165)** — two demographic-grounding tests had been red on any machine without `LLM_API_KEY`; they now mock the LLM client so the suite passes everywhere (6c5f0e7).
- **Neo4j 5.26 (#159)** — index reliability over the struggling 5.15 build.

## The engine stayed frozen
Worth saying plainly: across ten merged PRs, `simulation_runner` and `simulation_manager` saw zero edits. The whole week served the people *around* the sim — integrators who now get a verifiable, pollable, filterable output surface; contributors who now have a guide and proof one outsider can land a change; non-English readers who can finally read the thing. That's defensible work. A cheap simulation nobody can verify, consume, or stand up isn't worth much, and this week closed real gaps on all three.

But it's the second week running where the core sat still. The promise is "$1 to simulate **anything**" — not "$1 to read someone else's simulation." The edges are sharp now. Next week's real signal is whether the engine moves.
